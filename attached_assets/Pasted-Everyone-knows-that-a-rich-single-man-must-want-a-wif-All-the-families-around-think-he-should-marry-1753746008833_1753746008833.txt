Everyone knows that a rich single man must want a wif. All the families around think he should marry one of their daughter. One day, Mrs. Bennet said to her husband, "My dear M. Bennet, have you heard that Netherfield Park has a new owner?" Mr. Bennet said he had not heard this new. "But it is true," she sai. "Mrs. Long was just her. She told me all about i." "Do you want to know who bought it?" Mrs. Bennet aske. "You want to tell m. I will listen," Mr. Bennet sai. "Mrs. Long says a young rich man from the north bought i. He came on Monday in a fancy carriag. He loved the house right awa. He made a deal with M. Morris that da. He will move in before fal. Some of his servants will be there next wee." "What is his name?" Mr. Bennet aske. "Bingley." "Is he married?" "Oh! He is single, my dear! A single man with lots of mone. He makes four or five thousand a yea. What good news for our girls!" "How does this help them?" Mr. Bennet aske. "My dear M. Bennet," his wife said, "I think he might marry one of the." "Is that why he moved here?" "Of course not! But he might fall in love with one of our girl. So you must visit him when he come." "I do not need to do tha. You and the girls can g. You are as pretty as any of the. Maybe M. Bingley will like you bes." "My dear, you are too kin. When a woman has five grown daughters, she should not think about her own look." "But my dear, you must go see M. Bingley when he moves her." "I cannot promise that," Mr. Bennet sai. "Think about your daughter. Sir William and Lady Lucas will visit hi. They do not usually visit new peopl. You must go to. We cannot visit him if you do not go firs." "You worry too muc. Mr. Bingley will be happy to see yo. I will write him a not. I will tell him he can marry any of our girls he want. But I will put in a good word for my little Lizz." "Do not do that! Lizzy is not better than the other. She is not as pretty as Jan. She is not as fun as Lydi. But you always like her bes." "None of them have much to offer," he sai. "They are all silly like other girl. But Lizzy is smarter than her sister." "Mr. Bennet, how can you talk about your own children that way? You like to upset m. You do not care about my poor nerve." "You are wrong, my dea. I respect your nerves very muc. They are old friends of min. You have talked about them for twenty year."
